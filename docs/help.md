# Help & FAQ

Plain-language answers for students and parents.

## Do I need an account?
You can try the tutor as a guest and ask a few questions for free. To keep going and to **save your progress and goals**, you'll need to create an account — it's quick and free.

> Heads-up: the "5 free questions then sign up" experience is being rolled out. Right now guests and members share a fair-use limit of about **10 messages per hour**. Your progress and goals are only saved once you register.

## How do topics work?
Each chat is built around one maths topic — like Fractions, Decimals or Algebra. This keeps your learning focused and makes progress easy to see.

You're meant to keep up to **5 topics on the go** at once. If you've got 5 already and want to start something new, the tutor will suggest finishing or archiving one first, so you don't spread yourself too thin. *(Archiving is coming soon — for now this is a friendly nudge rather than a hard stop.)*

## How is my progress worked out?
Two things are tracked:
1. **Topic progress** — how far through a topic you are, shown like "Decimals – 45% complete". It goes up as you answer questions, cover new ideas, and show you understand them.
2. **Mastery** — a 0–5 score per topic the tutor saves to remember how secure you are (4–5 means you've basically got it).

## How do goals and exam prep work?
Tell the tutor a goal and a date — for example, "I've got a decimals test on 17 June." The tutor will set up an **Exam Prep: Decimals** focus and spend your sessions on practice questions, common exam-style problems, and the bits you find tricky — speeding up or slowing down depending on how much time is left.

## Why do I have to register after my free questions?
The free questions let you try things out. An account lets the tutor **remember you between sessions** — your topics, your progress, your goals and your exam dates — so every session picks up where you left off.

## What can the tutor help with?
Year 8 and Year 9 maths, building towards GCSE: number, algebra, ratio & proportion, geometry & measures, probability and statistics. It sticks to maths — that's its thing.
